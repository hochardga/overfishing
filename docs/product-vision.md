# Product Vision — Definitely Not Overfishing

## 1. Vision & Mission

### Vision Statement
Browser incrementals become known for delivering complete, emotionally resonant arcs instead of starting with a sharp premise and dissolving into hollow number growth.

### Mission Statement
Build a free browser game that starts as a cozy fishing toy and turns into an unsettling extraction-management satire by changing the player's role, incentives, and interface every 10-20 minutes.

### Founder's Why
Gerg is coming to games from the world of tools and apps, not from years of shipping genre products. That is a strength here, not a liability. The core design challenge in this project is not "make more content" or "make bigger numbers go up"; it is taking a complicated system and making its logic legible enough that players can feel themselves changing with it. Tool-builders tend to care about clarity, pacing, feedback, and understandable interfaces. This game needs exactly that discipline.

The sharper reason this founder is right for the project is the outsider angle. Most incrementals inherit genre habits without questioning them: add a new currency, add a new multiplier, stretch the grind, call it depth. Gerg's instinct is to ask whether the system is still saying anything. That is the right instinct for a game whose whole point is that optimization should feel compelling, readable, and eventually disturbing.

### Core Values

#### Role Change Over Raw Scale
Every phase must change what the player manages, not just how much they produce. If a feature only makes numbers rise faster, it has to fight for its place.

#### Systems Teach Theme
The satire should come from incentives, bottlenecks, and reward structures. The game should trust mechanics to teach the player what the world values before any text explains it.

#### Finish The First Run
The most important promise is a complete 75-90 minute arc. Anything that harms clarity, pacing, or closure in that first run is lower priority than it first appears.

#### Clarity Under Escalation
The game gets denser as it gets darker, but it must never become unreadable. A player should understand why they are winning even when the system has become morally ugly.

#### Ship The Prototype Publicly
This is not a stealth project that waits for perfection. The right move is to get a strong, playable, public prototype in front of real players and let their behavior refine the next version.

### Strategic Pillars

#### The Magic Moment Must Arrive Early Enough To Matter
The player's identity shift from fisher to operator should land by the end of the core MVP work, not as a late-game reward most people never see.

#### The Interface Tells Half The Story
The visual arc from soft coastal warmth to industrial severity is not polish. It is part of the narrative machinery and should be treated like a core system.

#### One New Tension Per Phase
The complexity curve should feel natural because each phase introduces one new management problem. Stacking multiple new tensions at once will make the game feel messy instead of revelatory.

#### Prototype Scope Is A Competitive Advantage
The smaller and more complete this version is, the more memorable it will be. A finished first-session experience beats a sprawling half-game.

### Success Looks Like
Twelve months from now, this project looks like a recognizable browser indie with a reputation for being the incremental people send to friends when they want to show the genre can still surprise them. The free prototype has reached at least 25,000 plays, more than 1,500 players have completed a full run or reached the License Renewal reset, and a small but reliable audience follows updates because the concept and execution both landed. At that point the business question is no longer "is this idea real?" but "does it stay a browser-first cult favorite, or expand into a larger premium release?"

## 2. User Research

### Primary Persona
Jordan is 34, works a knowledge-job schedule, and plays incrementals for mood and surprise as much as for optimization. They are comfortable with browser-based systems, happy to learn through play, and willing to give a free web game a real 60-90 minute session if the concept feels distinct. Their current habit is to bounce between idle games with clever openings, then drift away once the mechanics flatten or the theme stops evolving. They are not looking for infinite retention; they are looking for a memorable experience that feels smart enough to share.

What would make Jordan switch is not more content, but more cohesion. They want the loop, tone, UI, and progression to feel like parts of the same idea. If the game teaches itself cleanly and the emotional turn feels earned rather than preachy, they become the exact kind of player who posts a link with "this gets weird in a good way."

### Secondary Personas
Miles is 31 and more systems-driven than Jordan. He keeps a browser game open during breaks and cares deeply about whether late-game decisions introduce new tensions or merely compress time. He will forgive minimal art, but not shallow progression.

Avery is 28 and comes from management sims and strategy indies more than pure clickers. They are willing to try a browser incremental if the interface is readable and the concept has enough bite to justify the time investment. They are a bridge audience: if they stay, the game has escaped the incremental niche without betraying it.

Lena is a creator or highly social indie player who likes recommending strange, elegant web experiences. She is less likely to grind for efficiency than to respond to a strong transformation arc, a memorable visual shift, and an experience that can be explained in one line.

### Jobs To Be Done
Functionally, players want a browser game that teaches itself fast, introduces new tensions at the right pace, and rewards attention with meaningful system change rather than pure inflation. They want to feel forward motion within minutes and still feel novelty after an hour.

Emotionally, they want to feel clever, absorbed, and slightly unsettled by what they are choosing to optimize. The game should give them the satisfaction of mastery while quietly changing the moral context of that mastery.

Socially, they want a game that is worth recommending because it has a point of view. It is easier to tell a friend "you should try this fishing game that turns you into an extraction executive" than "this clicker has a lot of upgrades."

### Pain Points
The most severe pain point is thematic collapse. Many incrementals begin with a sharp premise, but later hours become generic production math that no longer feels connected to the fantasy. This happens often and is the main reason concept-driven players stop caring.

The second pain point is false depth. Players are asked to process more currencies, menus, and timers, but the actual decision space does not get richer. The game looks larger without becoming more interesting.

The third pain point is weak session design. A lot of browser incrementals are good for ten minutes, then ask for days of intermittent returns to discover whether there is anything else there. That rhythm works for retention-driven products, but it fails players who came for a finished arc.

The mildest but still relevant pain point is tonal laziness. Games that want to be funny, satirical, or dark often explain themselves too directly, making the player feel managed rather than implicated.

### Current Alternatives & Competitive Landscape
`Cookie Clicker` is still the reference point for mainstream browser incrementals because it is frictionless, legible, and endlessly expandable. What it does well is establish a loop instantly. What it does not do for this audience is keep transforming the player's role in a way that feels emotionally cumulative.

`Universal Paperclips` is closer to the real competitive target because it proves that a browser incremental can use escalation to say something bigger. Its strength is the speed with which theme and mechanics lock together. Its weakness for this product's niche is that the management fantasy is abstract from the start rather than evolving from something tactile and personal.

`A Dark Room` matters because it shows how mystery and revelation can carry lightweight interfaces. Its lesson is about restraint: the player does not need a huge UI to feel discovery. Its limitation here is that this game needs a more explicit tycoon layer and a more legible systems surface.

The biggest indirect alternative is "do nothing and open another short browser curiosity." That option is always available because free web games compete less on price than on attention and recommendation quality.

### Key Assumptions to Validate
We assume players will give a free browser incremental a full 75-90 minute session if the progression changes meaningfully every 10-20 minutes. To validate this, instrument phase reach and average session length during private and soft-launch testing.

We assume the fisher-to-operator role shift is the game's strongest shareable moment. To validate this, ask testers to describe the game in one sentence after their first session and see whether that shift appears unprompted.

We assume scarcity pricing will feel temptingly wrong rather than merely punitive or confusing. To validate this, track whether players notice price spikes and whether they exploit depleted regions instead of avoiding them.

We assume local-only saves and no auth are acceptable for the first public prototype. To validate this, watch how many complaints are specifically about device sync versus progression and balance.

We assume a gradual Soft-to-Severe visual transition is more effective than a sudden act break. To validate this, compare tester recall of the tone shift and whether they notice the UI hardening before it is pointed out.

We assume players want a complete first-run arc more than they want endless meta-systems. To validate this, measure completion satisfaction and collect feedback on whether the ending/reset feels like closure or truncation.

### User Journey Map
In awareness, Jordan hears about the game through a recommendation, an itch page, or a community post that frames it as a browser incremental with a real point of view. The first hurdle is skepticism; they have seen many "clever" web games that run out of ideas quickly.

In consideration, the one-line pitch and visual style matter. They need enough confidence that the game is readable, not sloppy, and that the first few minutes will teach the loop without wasting time.

During first use, the early dock loop must be immediately satisfying. Manual casting, warm UI, and simple upgrades create trust. The player needs to feel that the game respects their attention before it asks for deeper engagement.

The magic moment arrives when the player realizes that their best move is no longer to fish, but to direct fishing. That is the conversion point from "interesting prototype" to "I need to see where this goes."

Habit formation for this product is lighter than a live-service loop. It is less about daily returns and more about finishing a run, trying a better run, and following updates. Advocacy comes when the player can explain both the fantasy and the turn in a single sentence.

## 3. Product Strategy

### Product Principles
The game must always reward understanding, not clicking frequency. Manual interaction matters early because it teaches the system, not because the player should be trapped there forever.

Each unlock must change the decision surface. New layers should create new trade-offs among storage, routing, maintenance, processing, trust, or depletion rather than serving as fresh wrappers on the same calculation.

Numbers should stay interpretable. If the player cannot tell why a region is underperforming or why a bottleneck formed, the design has failed even if the system is technically deep.

The game should never lecture. Consequences belong in dashboards, price curves, copy, and incentives before they belong in overt narrative statements.

Closure is a feature. The License Renewal reset should feel like the end of a first chapter, not an arbitrary wall.

### Market Differentiation
Most browser incrementals choose one of two paths: they either stay lightweight and disposable, or they chase depth through ever more abstract production layers. Definitely Not Overfishing differentiates by keeping the emotional meaning of progression intact. The player does not merely get richer or busier; they become someone else. That matters because the target audience is tired of games whose premise is strongest at minute five and weakest at minute fifty. This design is defensible because the role-shift rule affects progression, UI, pacing, economy, copy, and launch messaging. It is not a theme skin; it is the product structure.

### Magic Moment Design
The magic moment is when the player realizes they have stopped being the fisher. For that moment to happen reliably, three things must be true. First, manual fishing must be satisfying enough early that the player forms an identity around it. Second, automation must become clearly superior for certain goals without making manual play irrelevant. Third, the UI and copy have to reinforce the transfer of agency by presenting routes, crews, and throughput as the smart next move.

The shortest path to that moment is to keep the opening loop compact, unlock skiff decisions quickly, then move into passive gear and a second boat before fatigue sets in. If the prototype cannot get a meaningful percentage of players to Fleet Ops within the planned first session, the MVP scope is wrong and should be cut back rather than padded with more content.

### MVP Definition
The MVP is the smallest version of the game that can deliver the full first-session arc. It includes a manual fishing opener, three clearly legible resource tensions before the role shift, a fleet phase that changes the player's job, a processing layer that turns catch into throughput, a regional extraction layer that exposes depletion and scarcity pricing, and a License Renewal reset that closes the run.

Done for MVP means more than "systems exist." Manual fishing must feel responsive, phase unlocks must happen at readable intervals, the Soft-to-Severe UI shift must be obvious in hindsight, and the final reset must feel like a conclusion with light carryover rather than a hard stop.

### Explicitly Out of Scope
Accounts, cloud saves, and any social layer are out of scope for v1 because they do nothing to prove the first-session arc. Reconsider them only if players explicitly ask to continue runs across devices or return in meaningful numbers.

Live-service content cadence is out of scope because it encourages breadth before the prototype has earned it. Revisit only after the first public version has stable completion and share rates.

Deep political systems, lobbying, and greenwashing mechanics are tempting because they are on-theme, but they belong after the core extraction arc works. The first version only needs enough trust and ocean-health pressure to make the system legible.

Monetization is out of scope. Do not add ads, currencies, or premium gates until the product has proven that people care enough to finish and recommend it.

### Feature Priority (MoSCoW)
**Must Have:** responsive manual fishing, phase-based progression, stock depletion and regeneration, scarcity pricing, passive gear, boats with routing and maintenance, processing and contracts, regional extraction signals, UI tone shift, local save support, License Renewal reset.

**Should Have:** event callouts for bottlenecks, lightweight analytics, exportable feedback links, optional settings for reduced motion and UI scale, balancing tables that are easy to tune.

**Could Have:** import/export saves, ambient audio layers that harden over the run, one optional late-session region, simple achievement callouts tied to role shifts.

**Won't Have This Time:** accounts, cloud saves, live events, monetization, deep policy simulation, mobile-native UX, multiple endings, or narrative cutscenes.

### Core User Flows
**Flow 1: First Run Start.** Trigger: player clicks `Play in browser`. They land in a warm dock interface, complete a few casts, buy a first upgrade, and feel the manual loop sharpen. Success means they understand cash, stock, and upgrade feedback within the first three minutes.

**Flow 2: Role Shift.** Trigger: the player reaches passive gear and fleet management. They compare manual yield to automated throughput, assign a route, and realize their attention is better spent managing boats than catching fish directly. Success means the player can explain what changed about their role without additional tutorial copy.

**Flow 3: License Renewal.** Trigger: the player reaches the late-session extraction layer and finishes the first run. They see a summary of damage, yield, and carryover, then choose to renew and begin again with small advantages. Success means the reset feels inevitable and enticing instead of punitive.

### Success Metrics
The primary metric is `% of players who reach Fleet Ops during their first session`. Below 25% means the early pacing is too slow or confusing. Around 35% is good. Above 45% is great and suggests the arc is teaching itself effectively.

Secondary metrics are `% of players who reach License Renewal`, `median session length`, `average phase reached`, and `% of players who return for a second run within seven days`. Good targets for the first public prototype are 15% reaching License Renewal, a median session length above 28 minutes, and 20% second-run return. Great would be 25%, 40 minutes, and 30% respectively.

Leading indicators matter because the audience is niche but expressive. Track feedback submissions, organic shares, and how often players describe the game using the words "role shift," "felt wrong," or "turns dark."

### Risks
The biggest risk is overbuilding before the emotional arc is proven. Likelihood is high because the theme invites more systems. Impact is high because sprawl will destroy pacing. Mitigation is ruthless scope control and phase-based validation.

The second risk is satire becoming explicit too early. Likelihood is medium. Impact is high because it would flatten the player's discovery. Mitigation is to express the critique through prices, bottlenecks, UI copy, and rewards first.

The third risk is weak late-game readability. Likelihood is medium. Impact is high because the role shift only works if the player understands the new job. Mitigation is a disciplined panel structure, clear alerts, and capped interface density.

The fourth risk is that players enjoy the concept but do not finish the session. Likelihood is medium. Impact is medium-high. Mitigation is milestone pacing, strong unlock cadence, and visible progress toward the License Renewal endpoint.

The fifth risk is audience mismatch. If the game becomes too punitive for idle fans or too light for management fans, it can miss both groups. Mitigation is to tune around the concept-first incremental audience first and let secondary audiences be a bonus.

The sixth risk is that local-only saves limit repeat play. Likelihood is low for the first prototype, but it grows after public release. Mitigation is to keep the save format clean so cloud sync can be layered in later if demand is real.

## 4. Brand Strategy

### Positioning Statement
For incremental players who want more than rising numbers, Definitely Not Overfishing is the browser management satire that turns a cozy fishing game into an unsettling extraction system. Unlike concept-first incrementals that flatten into abstract scaling, Definitely Not Overfishing keeps changing the player's job so progression feels mechanically and morally cumulative.

### Brand Personality
This brand is a charming host with a hidden agenda. Early on, it feels like someone inviting you onto a quiet dock at sunrise: warm, competent, lightly witty, and deeply approachable. Later it feels like that same person now wearing a windbreaker with a company logo, speaking in clean operational euphemisms, and acting as though everything is still perfectly normal. The brand never cackles, never shouts, and never breaks character for a cheap joke. It trusts contrast more than punchlines.

### Voice & Tone Guide

| Context | DO | DON'T |
|---|---|---|
| Onboarding | "Cast a line and see what's biting." | "Begin optimal marine extraction." |
| Error states | "Hold is full. Head back or unload first." | "You failed. Storage overflowed." |
| Empty states | "No active contracts yet. The board opens once your operation grows." | "Nothing here. Come back later." |
| Success messages | "Excellent yield. Harbor efficiency is improving." | "Nice! You exploited that region well." |
| Marketing copy | "A browser incremental where cozy fishing becomes an extraction machine." | "A hilarious anti-capitalist climate clicker." |

The voice is consistent: calm, competent, and slightly too polished once the game starts industrializing. Tone shifts with context, but it should never become frantic, jokey, or moralizing.

### Messaging Framework
**Tagline:** `Catch fish. Build fleets. Normalize collapse.`

**Homepage headline:** `A browser incremental that turns cozy fishing into industrial extraction.`

**Three value propositions:** First, it delivers a complete first-session arc instead of an endless vague grind. Second, each phase changes the player's role, so progression feels meaningful. Third, the satire is systemic: the game lets the player feel why destructive choices become rational.

**Feature descriptions:** Manual fishing is pitched as the tactile way in. Fleet routing is pitched as the moment the player's job changes. Processing and regional management are pitched as the point where "fish" becomes "throughput."

**Objection handlers:** If someone says "I don't usually play incrementals," the answer is that this is built as a contained browser experience, not a forever commitment. If someone says "I don't want a preachy political game," the answer is that the game never stops being a management toy; it just reveals what the toy rewards.

### Elevator Pitches
**5-second:** `It's a browser incremental where a cozy fishing game turns you into an extraction executive.`

**30-second:** `Definitely Not Overfishing starts like a warm little fishing clicker, then keeps changing your job. Before long you're managing boats, routes, processing lines, and collapsing regions. The joke is that the smartest business move keeps being the worst thing for the ocean, and the game never has to lecture you for that to land.`

**2-minute:** `Most browser incrementals have a good hook and then flatten into abstract number growth. Definitely Not Overfishing is built to fix that. You start on a dock catching fish by hand, buying small upgrades, and learning a satisfying loop. Then the game keeps evolving your role: you buy a skiff, manage fuel and storage, add passive gear, automate boats, process catch into products, and finally manage regional depletion and public pressure. The reason it sticks is that the theme and the systems never split apart. The more efficiently you play, the more the game reveals that efficiency has become the problem. It's a free web prototype first, designed to be finished in a real session and shared because the arc lands.`

### Competitive Differentiation Narrative
Competitors in this space usually win one of two ways: they are endlessly expandable number machines, or they are sharp one-idea experiments. Definitely Not Overfishing aims for the overlap. It uses browser-incremental readability and immediacy, but it structures progression so the player's identity changes with the system. That means the late game is not merely "more" of the early game; it is the payoff of the early game. That is why the premise should still feel strongest near the end of the first run, not weakest.

### Brand Anti-Patterns
Never let the product feel like generic idle sludge. That means no walls of tiny upgrade buttons, no rainbow economy clutter, and no dashboards that make the player work harder to understand the system than to play it.

Never let it become smug. The game can be sharp and unsettling, but it should not behave as though it is morally superior to the player for engaging with it.

Never reach for meme humor to carry the satire. The brand should feel intentional, not internet-random.

Never market it as a didactic issue game. The entry point is still pleasure, curiosity, and systems satisfaction.

## 5. Design Direction

### Design Philosophy
Design should preserve tactile trust before it introduces industrial pressure. The early game needs enough softness and warmth that the later hardening feels like a meaningful loss.

Information density should rise with player responsibility, not with arbitrary feature count. Denser late-game panels are correct only when the player's job has actually become more operational.

Surfaces, spacing, and typography should do the work of borders and chrome. The interface should feel layered and deliberate rather than outlined and boxy.

The game should always privilege fast scanning over decorative excess. Players should know where the pressure is: stock, bottlenecks, maintenance, or trust.

### Visual Mood
The right visual mood is coastal warmth corrupted by operational rigor. Early screens should feel like sunrise over painted wood, sea glass, and hand-labeled tackle. Late screens should feel like the same world has been repurposed into a fleet console: tighter spacing, colder surfaces, sharper edges, and warning accents. The reference blend is the warmth of the early-game UX notes crossed with the monolithic density of the end-game notes, connected by a gradual rather than theatrical transition.

Dark mode is not the default visual target for the prototype, but the late-game industrial panels effectively act as an internal dark theme. Use the industrial palette inside specific control surfaces rather than flipping the entire application at once.

### Typography
Use `Space Grotesk` for headings and major numeric callouts, `Plus Jakarta Sans` for body and UI copy, and `IBM Plex Mono` for rates, stock deltas, and diagnostic readouts. Load weights `500`, `600`, and `700` for Space Grotesk; `400`, `500`, and `600` for Plus Jakarta Sans; and `500` for IBM Plex Mono.

Set CSS variables to `--font-heading`, `--font-body`, and `--font-mono`. Use a type scale of `0.75rem`, `0.875rem`, `1rem`, `1.125rem`, `1.25rem`, `1.5rem`, `1.875rem`, and `2.5rem`. Keep body line-height at `1.5` minimum and headings between `1.1` and `1.2`.

### Spacing & Layout
Use a 4px base spacing system with tokens `4`, `8`, `12`, `16`, `24`, `32`, `48`, `64`, and `96`. Keep minimum spacing between adjacent controls at `8px`, between small stacked sections at `16px`, and between major dashboard sections at `24px` in early game and `20px` in late-game dense panels.

Max content width for the shell should be `1440px`. The game layout should use a 12-column CSS grid on desktop, collapsing to a single-column stacked layout under `960px`. Recommended breakpoints are `640px`, `960px`, and `1280px`.

### Component Philosophy
Cards should feel like layered materials rather than floating tiles. Early cards use radii between `16px` and `24px`; late industrial panels can tighten to `8px` or `4px`, but only after the interface has earned that hardness.

Avoid heavy borders. Prefer tonal separation between nested surfaces. Buttons should be pill-shaped or heavily rounded early, then move toward tighter radii for late-stage operational controls. Shadows should stay diffuse and tinted, never black and "app-like."

### Iconography & Imagery
Use an outline-forward icon set such as `lucide-react` with selectively heavier custom icons for boats, fish, fuel, storage, and contract categories. Early imagery should favor silhouette fish, hand-drawn chart motifs, and dockside textures. Late imagery should lean into route lines, telemetry ticks, manifests, and stock bars. Avoid stock photography entirely.

### Accessibility Commitments
Target WCAG 2.1 AA across all interactive UI. Maintain at least `4.5:1` contrast for body text and `3:1` for large text and charts paired with labels. Every interactive element must be keyboard reachable with a visible focus ring of at least `2px`. Minimum hit targets are `44px` on touch-capable devices. Include a reduced-motion setting, scalable text up to `200%`, and non-color indicators for stock danger and maintenance breakdown risk.

### Motion & Interaction
Default transition duration is `160ms` for hover and press states, `220ms` for panel reveals, and `320ms` for major phase-transition moments. Use easing curves close to `cubic-bezier(0.2, 0.8, 0.2, 1)`. Motion should suggest systems settling into place, not playful bounce. Phase unlocks can briefly feel ceremonial; repeated interactions should stay crisp and restrained.
