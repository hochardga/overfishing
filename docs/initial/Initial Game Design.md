# Overfishing: Initial Design

It will make the game much stronger if the loop does **not** stay “catch fish, buy bigger hook” forever.  
The best version is one where the player’s role changes over time:

**fisher \-\> boat owner \-\> fleet manager \-\> seafood executive \-\> oceanic planner**

That gives you the satire *and* the tycoon feel.

## **The core design rule**

Each phase should add **one new management problem**.

Do not just add more currencies. Add a new decision layer.

A good progression looks like this:

* **Phase 1:** timing and catching  
* **Phase 2:** storage and fuel  
* **Phase 3:** crew and boats  
* **Phase 4:** processing and contracts  
* **Phase 5:** regions, policy, and ecosystem collapse

So the game loop becomes more complex because the player has more systems to balance, not because the numbers get longer.

---

# **How the loop should evolve**

## **Phase 1 — Hand-to-Mouth Fishing**

**Fantasy:** you are a single person on a dock

### **Main loop**

**Cast \-\> catch \-\> sell \-\> buy gear**

### **Player actions**

* click to cast  
* hit a timing zone to catch  
* maybe choose bait  
* sell fish automatically at day end or instantly

### **Resources**

* Cash  
* Fish Nearby

### **Decisions**

Very simple:

* catch speed  
* catch value  
* bucket size

### **Tone**

Cozy, peaceful, local

This phase should feel almost like a tiny fishing toy.

---

## **Phase 2 — Small Boat Operator**

**Fantasy:** you now own a little boat and can stay out longer

### **New loop**

**Catch \-\> manage storage \-\> return to port \-\> sell \-\> refuel \-\> upgrade**

### **New systems**

* **Fuel**  
* **Storage**  
* **Distance from shore**

### **New management decisions**

* stay out longer for better fish, or return before spoilage  
* spend on bigger cooler or better engine  
* choose nearshore vs offshore fishing spots

### **Why this works**

Now the player is not just catching fish.  
They are managing a small operation.

### **Active/idle balance**

Still mostly active, but first idle tools appear:

* crab pots  
* passive lines  
* one helper

Those create background catch while the player still manually fishes.

---

## **Phase 3 — Multi-Boat Business**

**Fantasy:** you are no longer fishing, you are running fishing

This is where the game starts to become a light tycoon.

### **New loop**

**Assign boats \-\> consume fuel \-\> collect catches \-\> unload \-\> repair \-\> sell or store**

### **New systems**

* **Boats**  
* **Crew**  
* **Maintenance**  
* **Dock capacity**

### **New player decisions**

* buy a second boat or improve the first  
* send boats to different fishing grounds  
* hire cheap crew or efficient crew  
* spend on maintenance now or risk breakdowns  
* unload immediately or wait for better market prices

### **Resources**

* Cash  
* Fuel  
* Storage  
* Maintenance condition

### **New satire angle**

The player starts optimizing “throughput.” Fish begin turning into units of output.

### **Important change**

Manual casting should still exist, but now it becomes the **bonus play** rather than the main source of income.

The player actively fishes for:

* temporary boosts  
* rare catches  
* contract bonuses  
* special bait species

That keeps active play relevant even as idle expands.

---

## **Phase 4 — Processing and Logistics**

**Fantasy:** you are building a seafood company

This is the first true tycoon layer.

### **New loop**

**Catch \-\> transport \-\> process \-\> package \-\> fulfill contracts \-\> expand**

### **New systems**

* **Processing capacity**  
* **Cold storage**  
* **Product types**  
* **Contracts**

### **Why this matters**

Fish are no longer just sold raw.  
Now the player chooses what to do with them.

### **Example outputs**

* fresh fish  
* frozen fish  
* canned fish  
* fishmeal  
* premium export product

### **New management decisions**

* raw sale now vs processed sale later  
* build a freezer or a cannery  
* accept stable local demand or volatile export contracts  
* process low-value fish into fishmeal for volume  
* reserve premium species for luxury buyers

### **This is the moment the loop becomes:**

**resource conversion \+ logistics \+ scaling**

That is your tycoon heart.

### **Dark turn begins**

This is also where the satire sharpens:

* waste becomes a metric  
* bycatch can be rendered into product  
* scarcity makes premium species more valuable  
* the business starts preferring depletion

---

## **Phase 5 — Fleet Allocation and Regional Management**

**Fantasy:** you manage fleets across multiple waters

Now the game becomes a strategy-light operations sim.

### **New loop**

**Allocate fleets by region \-\> monitor stock health \-\> fulfill quotas/contracts \-\> manage crises \-\> reinvest**

### **New systems**

* **Ocean regions**  
* **Fish stocks**  
* **Bycatch**  
* **Habitat damage**  
* **Regional permits**  
* **Weather risk**

### **New player decisions**

* which region to exploit this week  
* whether to let one fishery recover  
* whether to chase high prices in collapsing areas  
* whether to risk illegal or borderline methods  
* whether to expand processing or fleet size

### **Tycoon feel**

You are now optimizing a portfolio:

* boats  
* facilities  
* routes  
* contracts  
* political influence

This is where the player fully becomes an executive rather than a fisher.

---

## **Phase 6 — Industrial Governance**

**Fantasy:** your company influences markets, regulation, and ocean policy

This is the late-game system layer.

### **New loop**

**Extract \-\> process \-\> influence \-\> suppress consequences \-\> expand extraction frontier**

### **New systems**

* **Influence**  
* **Public Trust**  
* **Subsidies**  
* **Regulation pressure**  
* **Ocean Health**

### **New player decisions**

* restore stocks or lobby for exemptions  
* invest in conservation or greenwashing  
* close a region temporarily or move to deeper waters  
* support stricter quotas publicly while expanding privately  
* fund research that slows backlash

### **Final satire**

The player stops solving “fishing problems” and starts solving “political obstacles to fishing harder.”

That is where the game gets dark.

---

# **The right complexity curve**

The biggest mistake would be unlocking everything too early.

Use this rule:

## **Every phase introduces only one new tension**

### **Phase 1 tension**

Can I catch enough?

### **Phase 2 tension**

Can I stay out long enough without wasting the trip?

### **Phase 3 tension**

Can I scale boats without fuel and repairs eating profit?

### **Phase 4 tension**

Can my facilities handle what I catch?

### **Phase 5 tension**

Can I keep fleets profitable without collapsing regions?

### **Phase 6 tension**

Can I preserve profit while the ecosystem and public turn against me?

That makes the growth feel natural.

---

# **How to make it feel like a tycoon game**

To get the “light resource management with tycoon elements” feeling, you want **conversion chains** and **capacity bottlenecks**.

## **1\. Conversion chains**

Fish should stop being just money.

Example:

**Raw Catch \-\> Cold Storage \-\> Processing \-\> Product \-\> Contract Revenue**

That is the core tycoon pattern.

Later:

**Bycatch \-\> Fishmeal \-\> Feed \-\> Aquaculture Boost \-\> More Output**

That is dark, efficient, and very on-theme.

## **2\. Capacity bottlenecks**

At each stage, the player should hit a different limit:

* boat hold is full  
* fuel tank is too small  
* dock unload is too slow  
* freezer is maxed  
* processing plant is backed up  
* trucks are delayed  
* market demand is saturated

Those bottlenecks create management gameplay.

## **3\. Portfolio choices**

Give the player several competing investments:

* buy another boat  
* improve current boat  
* expand dock  
* build freezer  
* improve sonar  
* take a contract  
* buy PR  
* buy quota rights

That makes the player feel like they are running a business.

# **Recommended system ladder**

Here is a clean version of what should unlock when.

## **Layer 1: Personal fishing**

* cast line  
* catch  
* sell

## **Layer 2: Trip management**

* fuel  
* storage  
* offshore spots

## **Layer 3: Passive gear**

* crab pots  
* longlines  
* assistant fisher

## **Layer 4: Boat management**

* multiple boats  
* crew wages  
* repairs

## **Layer 5: Facility management**

* dock  
* freezer  
* cannery  
* fishmeal plant

## **Layer 6: Market management**

* local sales  
* wholesalers  
* exports  
* contracts

## **Layer 7: Regional management**

* fishing grounds  
* stock levels  
* closures  
* weather

## **Layer 8: Political management**

* regulations  
* lobbying  
* public trust  
* sustainability branding

That sequence naturally grows from active to idle to tycoon.

# **A good “role shift” for the player**

This is important psychologically.

## **Early game**

“I caught that fish.”

## **Mid game**

“My boats caught that fish.”

## **Late game**

“My system produced that tonnage.”

## **Endgame**

“My industry extracted that biomass.”

That loss of intimacy is part of the satire.

# **Concrete example of how one run evolves**

## **First 10 minutes**

* active fishing  
* bucket upgrades  
* bait upgrades  
* tiny passive traps

## **10–20 minutes**

* first boat  
* fuel and storage matter  
* return-to-port decisions  
* one helper  
* first passive earnings are meaningful

## **20–40 minutes**

* second and third boats  
* crew costs  
* repairs  
* docking bottlenecks  
* first processing upgrade  
* player starts routing fish into products

## **40–90 minutes**

* fleet assignment by region  
* processor and freezer balancing  
* contract fulfillment  
* fish stock depletion becomes visible  
* bycatch and habitat penalties begin to matter

## **Later**

* corporate scale  
* region management  
* market manipulation  
* political systems  
* ocean collapse mitigation just enough to continue

# **The best way to keep active play relevant**

When idle and tycoon systems take over, active play should not disappear. It should become **intervention play**.

Instead of manual casting being the main loop forever, the player actively does things like:

* resolve port bottlenecks  
* respond to storms  
* review scandals  
* manually target rare shoals  
* negotiate contracts  
* rebalance fleet routes  
* emergency harvest during price spikes

So the active layer evolves from “catch fish” to “manage crises and opportunities.”

That keeps the player engaged.

# **A strong central loop for the whole game**

If I compress the full game into one sentence, the evolving loop should be:

**Harvest fish \-\> manage capacity \-\> convert catches into products \-\> fulfill contracts \-\> expand infrastructure \-\> absorb ecological damage \-\> keep scaling**

That gives you both incremental growth and tycoon texture.

---

# **Strongest recommendation**

Do **not** make the loop more complex by just adding 8 resources.  
Make it more complex by adding these four kinds of decisions, one at a time:

* **where to fish**  
* **what to do with the catch**  
* **how to allocate fleet and facilities**  
* **how to manage the consequences**

That will feel much better than a bloated idle game.
