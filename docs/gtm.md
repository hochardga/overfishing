# Go-to-Market — Definitely Not Overfishing

## 1. Market Context
There is a real audience for browser-playable incremental experiments, but that audience is selective. The opportunity is not "all web players"; it is the overlap between incremental players who still want discovery, indie players who like systems with a point of view, and social players who enjoy sending around weird, clever browser experiences. That overlap is smaller than the mainstream idle audience, but it is exactly the group most likely to reward a complete first-session arc.

Why now: browser friction is still the easiest way to get someone into an unusual game, concept-first incrementals continue to attract attention when they say something new, and communities around itch.io and incremental-game spaces still make room for browser prototypes. The New Years Incremental Game Jam 2026 page explicitly shows a browser-heavy submission base and an active Discord/community loop, which is a useful signal that the format is alive, not nostalgic. The recurring Feedback Friday threads in r/incremental_games suggest there is still appetite for work-in-progress critique, but that community is strict about genre fit, so launch positioning has to be honest and legible.

## 2. Launch Strategy
The launch should happen in three steps: quiet pre-launch prep, a narrow soft launch for signal quality, then a public release push once the first-session arc is stable. This matches the founder's stated preference to let the finished prototype earn attention rather than marketing the making-of process.

Pre-launch is about clarity and readiness, not audience theater. Build the game, instrument phase reach, prepare the pitch assets, and recruit a small test group who will actually finish a run or tell you why they bounced.

Soft launch is about signal quality. Release first to places where people understand incrementals and browser prototypes, collect honest feedback on pacing and the role shift, and patch the obvious breakpoints quickly.

Public launch is about distribution and framing. Once the prototype can reliably land the dark turn, push it to broader channels with a one-line hook, a strong GIF or screenshot sequence, and a clear browser-play CTA.

## 3. Pre-Launch Playbook
**Week -8:** Lock the MVP scope, confirm the public launch date, and define the single metric that matters most: reach to Fleet Ops and License Renewal. Anything not supporting that metric should be cut or deferred.

**Week -7:** Publish a simple coming-soon presence. This can be a lightweight itch draft page or a minimal landing page with the one-line pitch, two screenshots, and an email capture form through an external service such as Buttondown or Beehiiv. Do not over-engineer this inside the game repo.

**Week -6:** Create the first launch asset pack: a 15-20 second GIF of the cozy dock phase, a second GIF showing the late operational UI, a square key image, and a short FAQ. The marketing job here is to make the transformation legible in seconds.

**Week -5:** Recruit 10-15 private testers. Prioritize one-third incremental fans, one-third systems/strategy players, and one-third general indie friends with taste. Ask them to record session length, furthest phase reached, and the sentence they would use to describe the game.

**Week -4:** Run the first closed test wave. Do not ask "did you like it?" Ask: where did you stop, what confused you, when did the role shift land, and would you send this to a friend?

**Week -3:** Patch pacing problems and add missing instrumentation. Your job is to remove blockers to the first complete run, not to add more content. Update the asset pack once the visual arc feels final enough to show.

**Week -2:** Prepare public release surfaces. Finalize the itch.io page, write the launch post variants for Reddit and social, assemble a one-page press kit, and make sure the landing copy matches the actual game.

**Week -1:** Do a final bug bash and soft-launch readiness check. Make sure analytics, feedback links, save recovery, and the first-run finish path all work. Schedule posts in drafts, not in tools; keep room to adapt to last-minute fixes.

## 4. Launch Week Plan
**Day 1:** Publish the game on itch.io and your own landing route if both are ready. Post first to the most relevant niche audience, not the broadest one: incremental-focused channels and communities that understand what the game is trying to do.

**Day 2:** Review analytics and tester feedback. If players are failing before the skiff or passive-gear phase, do not widen distribution yet. Patch the biggest pacing or clarity issue first.

**Day 3:** Share the strongest before-and-after asset set: one warm early screenshot, one late operational screenshot, and one sentence about the role shift. This is the best day for a short thread or postmortem-style teaser.

**Day 4:** Post to broader feedback communities such as r/playmygame or other browser-game-friendly spaces if the soft-launch response is stable. Lead with the playable link and one-line concept, not a long dev diary.

**Day 5:** Reach out directly to 10-20 people who cover, curate, or simply enjoy strange browser games. Do not mass pitch. Send a short, personal note with the browser link, one-line premise, and why it might fit their taste.

**Day 6:** Publish a brief "what players are noticing" update based on real reactions. This creates social proof without pretending the game is bigger than it is.

**Day 7:** Review the week against the actual goals: plays, phase reach, completion, feedback quality, and organic shares. Decide whether the next two weeks are about polishing the same prototype or starting a larger roadmap.

## 5. Post-Launch Growth
Weeks 1-2 should focus on fixing the first obvious breakpoints: confusing unlocks, unreadable bottlenecks, or phase pacing that drags. Release patches quickly and visibly so early players feel momentum rather than abandonment.

Weeks 3-4 should focus on recommendation quality. Ask players what sentence they use to describe the game and what screenshot or moment made them share it. Improve the landing page, itch page, and screenshots based on that language.

Weeks 5-8 should focus on second-run motivation. If the first License Renewal works, start tuning carryover bonuses, clarity of the reset summary, and the reasons players might immediately try again. If second-run behavior is weak, do not add breadth; improve closure and re-entry.

Weeks 9-12 should determine direction. If the game is being recommended for its concept and arc, double down on polish, clarity, and one or two carefully chosen post-launch features. If players like the premise but not the session, revisit pacing before expanding scope.

## 6. Channel Strategy
**1. itch.io HTML5 release page and incremental tags**  
This is the highest-ROI home base because it supports browser play, screenshots, devlogs, comments, and iterative updates in one place. Effort is moderate and the return is durable because the page continues to serve as a canonical link. Timeline to results is immediate on launch day and ongoing over weeks.

**2. r/incremental_games, especially structured feedback threads**  
This is the highest-signal niche audience, but it is not a casual self-promo dump. The community's removal behavior suggests you need to position the game clearly as an incremental and participate in good faith. Effort is low to medium; return is high if the game truly fits and you ask for specific feedback.

**3. Incremental Game Jam community / Discord orbit**  
The 2026 jam page shows an active browser-first community and Discord presence. Even if you are not launching through a jam, the surrounding community is likely to understand balance, pacing, and novelty better than general indie spaces. Effort is medium because it requires participation, not a drive-by link. Return is high for feedback quality, moderate for raw traffic.

**4. r/playmygame and adjacent feedback-driven communities**  
This is a useful broader audience once the prototype is stable enough to survive outside the niche. Effort is low. Return is moderate: good for extra eyes and some wish-list-style interest, weaker for deep incremental-specific critique.

**5. IndieDB project page and devlog posts**  
Lower ROI than itch for a tiny browser prototype, but still useful if you want a persistent external profile and occasional discovery through indie-game browsing. Effort is moderate. Return is slower and more reputation-oriented than immediate.

## 7. Content Strategy
The highest-performing content for this game will be transformation content, not raw feature lists. Show the same interface at minute 3 and minute 70. Show the player's job changing. Show stock bars falling while prices rise. That is the hook.

Publish in three repeating formats. First, short visual comparisons: cozy dock versus industrial operations. Second, concise postmortems: one design lesson about pacing, role shifts, or system clarity. Third, player-language highlights: quotes or paraphrases that prove the satire is landing through play.

Cadence for a solo founder should stay realistic: one meaningful post per major patch, one launch-week burst, and then one insight or clip every 1-2 weeks while the prototype is still moving.

## 8. Community Strategy
Show up as a participant before you show up as a promoter. In incremental communities, the fastest way to lose trust is to drop a link without demonstrating that you understand the genre conversation. Comment on other projects, share what you're tuning, and ask targeted questions.

Use community participation mainly for learning and credibility, not for brute-force traffic. A useful pattern is to ask for feedback on one specific thing: pacing to Phase 4, scarcity readability, or whether the License Renewal feels earned. That invites stronger responses than "what do you think?"

If you join the jam-adjacent Discord/community spaces, contribute progress and questions, not only launch announcements. Those communities can become long-term allies if they see you iterating seriously.

## 9. Key Metrics
Acquisition target for the first 90 days: `300+ total players`, with at least `150` arriving from organic community posts or recommendations rather than direct friends-and-family traffic.

Activation target: `70%` of players complete at least five manual casts, `45%` purchase an upgrade, and `35%` reach Fleet Ops. Those numbers indicate the early loop is teaching itself and the magic moment is attainable.

Retention target: `20%` of players start a second run within seven days. Because this is a contained prototype, returning once is more meaningful than daily use.

Completion target: `15%` of players reach License Renewal in the public prototype phase. Stretch target: `25%`.

Revenue target: none. Replace revenue with audience-quality signals: at least `20` detailed written feedback responses, `50` meaningful community comments/replies across launch channels, and a small mailing list of `100+` people interested in updates.

## 10. Budget Considerations
Keep the budget lean. The product itself should be cheap to host, so the main costs are likely to be a domain, optional analytics, and any small art/audio purchases you make to raise polish.

Reasonable solo-founder budget:
- Domain: `$12-20/year`
- Hosting: `$0`
- Analytics/error reporting: `$0-20/month`
- Art/audio polish budget: `$0-300 one-time`, only if it directly improves the first-run arc

The first place to invest money is not ads. It is clarity: better screenshots, one stronger GIF, one cleaner logo/cover image, or one targeted piece of polish that makes the transformation easier to understand.

## 11. Risks
**Risk: The concept is stronger than the session.**  
Mitigation: measure phase reach and completion before expanding distribution. Do not confuse clicks with a working product.

**Risk: Community posts are removed or ignored because the genre fit is unclear.**  
Mitigation: pitch the game honestly as a browser incremental and use channels such as Feedback Friday where critique is expected.

**Risk: Launching too broadly before the role shift is stable creates the wrong first impression.**  
Mitigation: use a soft launch first and patch the first-run blockers quickly.

**Risk: The satire is misread as preachy or smug.**  
Mitigation: keep launch messaging focused on the experience and the role shift, not on claiming moral superiority.

**Risk: Session length is too long for a browser audience.**  
Mitigation: instrument drop-off by phase and cut or compress anything that delays Fleet Ops or the first meaningful contract layer.

**Risk: The founder spreads attention across too many channels.**  
Mitigation: prioritize itch.io, one high-signal incremental channel, and one broader feedback channel before doing anything else.
